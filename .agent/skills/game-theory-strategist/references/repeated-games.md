# Repeated Games & Signaling
## Reputation, Long-Term Competitive Dynamics & Information Games

---

## Why Repetition Changes Everything

In a one-shot game, defection can be rational — there's no tomorrow to worry about. When a game is repeated (indefinitely or with uncertain end date), cooperation can emerge as a stable equilibrium because players care about future interactions.

**The Folk Theorem**: In infinitely repeated games, any individually rational outcome can be sustained as an equilibrium if players are patient enough (discount factor δ is high enough). This is why markets with long-term players develop norms, reputations, and implicit cooperation — even without contracts.

**Business implication**: If you expect to interact with a counterparty repeatedly, defecting for short-term gain is rarely optimal. Your reputation is an asset. Competitors, investors, customers, and partners all maintain mental models of how you behave.

---

## Tit for Tat and Competitive Equilibria

In repeated Prisoner's Dilemmas (price wars, feature races), **Tit for Tat** is empirically the most robust cooperative strategy:
1. Cooperate on the first interaction
2. Mirror whatever the opponent did last round
3. Return to cooperation immediately after the opponent cooperates again

**Why it works**: It's nice (cooperates first), retaliatory (punishes defection), forgiving (doesn't hold grudges), and clear (opponent can predict exactly what you'll do).

**Competitive market application**: 
- Match competitor price cuts immediately but don't initiate them
- Signal willingness to hold prices when markets are stable
- Be predictable — erratic behavior destroys cooperative equilibria
- Publicly communicate your strategy ("we will always match cuts but never initiate them") to coordinate on the cooperative outcome

---

## Grim Trigger Strategy

More severe than Tit for Tat: cooperate until the opponent defects once, then defect forever.

**When to use**: When the one-time gain from defection is very high and you want maximum deterrence. Works best when:
- Both parties have long time horizons (high discount factor)
- Defection is observable and verifiable
- The relationship is valuable enough that permanent punishment is credible

**Risk**: Grim Trigger is fragile to misunderstanding. Mistaken defection triggers permanent punishment, destroying otherwise cooperative relationships. Build in a renegotiation clause.

## Reputation as a Strategic Asset

Reputation is the accumulated signal of how you've played past games. It affects:

**Credibility of threats**: If you have a history of carrying out threats, future threats are believed. If you've backed down repeatedly, threats are discounted.

**Partner selection**: Counterparties with good reputations get better terms — lower cost of capital, better distribution deals, stronger talent pipeline.

**Competitive deterrence**: Incumbents sometimes fight unprofitably against small entrants to maintain a reputation for toughness that deters larger future entrants.

**Managing your competitive reputation**:
- Respond consistently to competitive moves so opponents can predict you
- Choose battles strategically — fight hard in your core segments, accommodate entry in peripheral ones
- Be visible about your commitments before competitors make moves

## Signaling Theory

In markets with asymmetric information, high-quality players need to distinguish themselves from low-quality players. A **signal** is a costly action that low-quality players would not find worth taking — this cost is what makes it credible.

### Spence's Education Model (adapted for business)

Original insight: Education signals ability not just because it builds skills, but because it's costly — and more costly for low-ability workers. This makes it a credible signal.

**Business equivalents**:

| Signal | Why it's credible |
|--------|------------------|
| Raising venture capital at a valuation | VCs do due diligence; acceptance signals quality |
| Offering a money-back guarantee | Only confident companies can afford to offer this |
| Publishing open-source code | Low-quality code can't hide; only works if code is good |
| Hiring away a competitor's top engineers | Only high-trajectory companies can attract talent |
| Long-term customer contracts | Only confident in retention can commit to this |
| Sharing detailed metrics publicly | Only strong performers benefit from transparency |

**Design test for a signal**: Would a low-quality competitor find it too expensive or too risky to copy? If yes, the signal is credible.

## Screening: Revealing Private Information

Where signaling has the informed party act first, **screening** has the uninformed party design options that cause the informed party to self-reveal through their choices.

**Classic example**: Airline pricing. Business travelers have high willingness to pay and low price elasticity. Airlines design restrictions (advance purchase, Saturday stay, non-refundable) that are unattractive to high-WTP travelers, causing them to self-select into premium fares.

**Startup applications**:

*Pricing tiers*: Design your pricing so that different customer segments self-select. A free tier + paid tier isn't just marketing — it screens for different willingness-to-pay cohorts and reveals information about segment sizes.

*Hiring*: Work samples and take-home projects screen for ability and motivation. Candidates who won't complete them reveal their type.

*Pilot programs*: Before full partnership, propose a small paid pilot. Partners who resist reveal they're either not serious or not confident in their execution.

*Investor screening*: Founders who take meetings with 50 investors but close only from those who move fast are screening for conviction.

## Dynamic Competitive Signaling

**Predatory pricing** (or the threat of it): Price below cost to signal to potential entrants that entry will not be profitable. Only credible if:
- You have deeper pockets than potential entrants (can sustain losses longer)
- Entrants believe you'll actually follow through (requires reputation for toughness)
- Legal in your jurisdiction (often isn't — consult counsel)

**Limit pricing**: Price just below the level that makes entry attractive. Doesn't require fighting; just requires keeping profitability invisible to entrants.

**Quality signaling in new markets**: In markets where buyers can't observe quality before purchase (experience goods), quality signals matter enormously. Price high (counterintuitively) — low prices signal low quality in unestablished markets. Invest in prestigious early customers whose adoption signals to the broader market.

## Worked Example: Enterprise SaaS Competitive Response

**Situation**: Established enterprise SaaS ($15M ARR) facing a new entrant with aggressive pricing (40% below market).

**Repeated game analysis**:
- This is not a one-shot game — both companies will interact repeatedly across renewal cycles and prospect pipelines
- Matching the price cut signals to the market that the lower price is sustainable, legitimizes the entrant
- **Grim Trigger**: Match cuts immediately and publicly — entrant must believe there's no margin in price competition

**Signaling play**: Instead of matching price, respond by:
1. Publicly announcing a "Premium Support" tier with tangible commitments (signals confidence in retention)
2. Accelerating releases of features the entrant cannot yet build (signals product velocity)
3. Securing 3-year renewals with current customers at existing pricing (reduces available market for entrant)

**Reputation strategy**: Do not compete on price in segments where the entrant focuses. Accommodate entry in low-margin SMB segments. Fight hard in enterprise where long-term reputation for premium service is an asset.

**Expected outcome**: Entrant either raises prices (runs out of runway subsidizing low pricing) or successfully segments into SMB while you retain enterprise — a market structure you can live with.
