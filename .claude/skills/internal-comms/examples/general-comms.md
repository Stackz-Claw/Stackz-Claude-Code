## Instructions

You are being asked to write an internal communication that doesn't fit the standard formats (3P updates, newsletters, FAQs, or incident reports). This is the most flexible format — it covers announcements, leadership messages, project updates, org changes, policy changes, all-hands prep, and anything else employees need to know.

---

## Clarifying Questions (Ask Before Writing)

If the request is ambiguous, get answers to these before drafting:

1. **Who is the audience?** (All-company? A specific team? Leadership only? Cross-functional group?)
2. **What channel will this go in?** (Slack? Email? A doc? An all-hands slide?)
3. **What's the primary goal?** (Inform? Inspire action? Reassure? Explain a decision?)
4. **What tone?** (Formal announcement? Casual team update? Urgent? Celebratory?)
5. **Is there a call to action?** (What do you want people to do after reading this?)

You don't need all five answered — but audience and goal are the minimum before writing well.

---

## Format Selection by Communication Type

### Announcement (new hire, promotion, org change, policy)
```
[One sentence: what is being announced]

[1–2 sentences of context: why this is happening or why it matters]

[If it affects people's work: what changes for them specifically]

[Call to action or next step, if any]
— [Sender name/team]
```

### Leadership Message / Executive Note
```
[Warm but direct opener — name the topic immediately]

[Core message in 1–3 paragraphs: what happened, what it means, what comes next]

[Acknowledgment of the team's work or difficulty of situation, where genuine]

[Clear close: what you're asking of people, or what they can expect from you]
— [Name, Title]
```

### Project Update (mid-flight)
```
**[Project Name] Update — [Date or Sprint]**

**Status**: [On track 🟢 / At risk 🟡 / Blocked 🔴]

**This week**: [What was accomplished — specific, data-backed where possible]

**Next week**: [What's planned — prioritized, not exhaustive]

**Needs/blockers**: [What the team needs from others, if anything]

**Links**: [Design doc, tracker, dashboard — the 1–2 most useful links only]
```

### Org Change / Reorg Communication
This is high-stakes. Employees read between the lines. Prioritize clarity about what changes and what doesn't.

```
[Direct opening: what is changing and when it takes effect]

[Why: the business reason — employees deserve the real reason, not corporate language]

[What this means for [specific groups]: address the most-affected people explicitly]

[What is NOT changing: often the most reassuring part]

[What happens next: timeline, who to talk to, where to ask questions]
— [Name, Title]
```

### All-Hands / Town Hall Prep
Structure the narrative before worrying about slides:
1. **Opening**: What's the mood/context we're acknowledging?
2. **Where we are**: Key metrics, milestones, honest assessment
3. **What we learned**: Wins, misses, surprises from this period
4. **Where we're going**: Priorities for the next period (3 max)
5. **What we need from the team**: One clear ask
6. **Q&A**: Surface the questions people are afraid to ask in the room

## Tone Guide

| Situation | Tone | Watch out for |
|---|---|---|
| All-company announcement | Professional, warm, inclusive | Jargon, assuming context, passive voice |
| Leadership message | Direct, human, clear | Corporate hedging ("we believe," "it is our intention") |
| Difficult news (layoffs, missed targets) | Honest, calm, respectful | Minimizing, over-explaining, defensive language |
| Celebration / win | Energetic, specific, genuine | Vague praise ("the team was amazing"), leaving people out |
| Urgent action needed | Clear, calm, specific | Alarmist language, unclear asks |
| Policy change | Matter-of-fact, helpful | Legalistic tone, burying the so-what |

---

## Universal Editing Checklist

Before presenting output, run through this:

- [ ] **First sentence passes the skim test** — the point is clear without reading further
- [ ] **No passive voice** where active works ("was decided" → "we decided")
- [ ] **No corporate hedging** ("leverage," "synergies," "circle back," "at the end of the day")
- [ ] **Acronyms defined** on first use for cross-functional audiences
- [ ] **CTA is specific** — "let me know if you have questions" is not a CTA; "reply with your preference by Friday" is
- [ ] **Length matches the channel** — Slack shorter than email; email shorter than a doc
- [ ] **Tone matches the moment** — a restructuring announcement and a product launch read differently

## Worked Example: Org Change Announcement

**Situation**: Two teams are merging. Manager wants a Slack message to both teams.

> Hi everyone — I have an update to share about our team structure.
>
> Starting March 10, the Data Platform and Analytics Engineering teams are merging into a single team: Data & Analytics. I'll be the manager for the combined group, with Priya continuing to lead the analytics track and Marcus continuing to lead platform infrastructure.
>
> **Why we're doing this**: As our data stack has grown, the boundary between platform and analytics work has become increasingly blurry. A unified team will move faster, reduce handoff friction, and give everyone more visibility into the full data stack.
>
> **What changes**: Team channel becomes #data-analytics (merging #data-platform and #analytics-eng). Sprint planning moves to Wednesdays starting March 13. Job ladders, comp, and reporting lines are unchanged.
>
> **What doesn't change**: Your projects, your day-to-day work, your teammates. This is a structural simplification, not a strategic pivot.
>
> Combined team kickoff: March 10 at 10 AM PT — calendar invite going out today. Bring questions.
>
> — Jordan

**Why it works**: States the change clearly, explains the real reason, directly addresses what changes vs. what doesn't, ends with a specific next step.
